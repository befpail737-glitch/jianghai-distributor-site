Understanding ESR and Its Impact on SMPS Efficiency

Published: February 20, 2025 | By: Jane Smith, FAE | Category: Application Note

Equivalent Series Resistance (ESR) is one of the most critical parameters to consider when selecting capacitors for Switch-Mode Power Supplies (SMPS). While capacitance gets most of the attention, ESR often determines whether your power supply will meet efficiency targets or suffer from thermal issues.

What is ESR?

ESR represents the total resistance in series with an ideal capacitor. It includes the resistance of the electrolyte, foil, tabs, and terminals. In an AC application like SMPS output filtering, this resistance causes power dissipation according to the formula P = I²R, where I is the RMS ripple current.

Impact on Efficiency

In a typical SMPS, output capacitors must handle significant ripple current. High ESR means more power loss as heat, which directly reduces overall efficiency. For example, a capacitor with 100mΩ ESR handling 5A RMS ripple current will dissipate 2.5W just in the capacitor!

Thermal Considerations

The power dissipated due to ESR creates heat inside the capacitor, raising its core temperature above ambient. This temperature rise accelerates the evaporation of electrolyte, significantly reducing capacitor life. High ESR can create a destructive cycle: more heat leads to higher ESR, which leads to even more heat.

Practical Design Tips

When selecting capacitors for SMPS applications:

  1. Calculate the RMS ripple current accurately using your switching frequency and load conditions
  2. Choose capacitors with ESR ratings that keep power dissipation under 1W for good thermal management
  3. Consider paralleling multiple smaller capacitors instead of one large one to reduce total ESR
  4. Ensure adequate airflow around high-current output capacitors
